# Logos Core Examples

## Example 1: Dashboard Frustration

Input:

> We built a dashboard for operations, but nobody uses it. Leadership keeps asking why the numbers are off, and the team says the data is not trustworthy.

Weak response:

> Improve the dashboard by making it more user-friendly and ensuring better data quality.

Logos response:

```text
Signal:
The dashboard is exposing disagreement about how the operation is actually run.

Pattern:
Leadership wants visibility, but the team does not trust the data or the follow-up process.

Real issue:
This is not only a dashboard problem. It is an ownership and decision rhythm problem.

Decision:
Decide which metrics matter, who owns each one, and where exceptions get reviewed.

Action:
1. Pick the 5 operating metrics that matter most.
2. Assign one owner to each metric.
3. Define what counts as an exception.
4. Review exceptions weekly with the people who can act.

Owner:
Operations leader owns the rhythm. Data owner supports accuracy.

Rhythm:
Weekly 30-minute operating review.

Artifact:
Metric ownership map with definition, owner, exception threshold, and follow-up action.

Review:
After 4 weeks, check whether decisions are happening faster, not whether the dashboard has more views.
```

## Example 2: Too Many Priorities

Input:

> Our team has too many priorities. Everyone is busy, but nothing important seems to move.

Logos response:

```text
Signal:
The team is confusing activity with movement.

Pattern:
Work is being accepted faster than decisions are being made.

Real issue:
This is a priority ownership problem.

Decision:
Decide what the team will protect, what it will pause, and who has authority to say no.

Action:
1. Name the top 3 outcomes for the next 30 days.
2. Pause or defer anything that does not support those outcomes.
3. Assign one accountable owner per outcome.
4. Review progress every Friday with blockers and decisions, not status theater.

Owner:
Department leader owns priority tradeoffs.

Rhythm:
Weekly 30-minute priority review.

Artifact:
30-day priority sheet: outcome, owner, next action, blocker, decision needed.

Review:
At the end of 30 days, measure completed outcomes, not completed tasks.
```

## Example 3: Vague Request

Input:

> Help us get better at follow-through.

Logos response:

```text
Signal:
Follow-through is failing because commitments are not visible enough to be managed.

Pattern:
The team likely leaves meetings with discussion, not decisions.

Real issue:
This is a rhythm and ownership gap.

Decision:
Decide where commitments will live and who reviews them.

Action:
1. End every meeting with owner, action, due date, and review point.
2. Keep one visible commitment list.
3. Start each recurring meeting by reviewing prior commitments.
4. Remove or escalate stuck items.

Owner:
Meeting owner owns the commitment list.

Rhythm:
Review commitments at the start of each recurring meeting.

Artifact:
Commitment tracker.

Review:
After 3 meetings, check whether fewer actions are being carried over without a decision.
```
